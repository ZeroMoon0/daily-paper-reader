# A Theory on Flow Matching with Neural Networks 

Yihan He *, 1 Qishuo Yin *, 1 Yuan Cao 2 Jianqing Fan 1 Han Liu 3

Abstract 

In this work, we develop theoretical foundation for flow matching with neural-network‚Äďparameterized conditional velocity fields. We establish convergence guaran-tees for gradient descent in the over-parameterized 2-layered ReLU neural network regime. We derive generalization bounds for the conditional velocity-field matching objective. Building on these results, we provide Wasserstein-distance guarantees for the samples generated by the induced flow. Our analysis is based on generalization bound for multi-task representation learning with unbounded losses, which may be of independent interest beyond flow-based generative modeling. These theoretical results are validated through extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world image benchmarks. 

# 1 Introduction 

Flow matching [Lipman et al., 2022, Albergo and Vanden-Eijnden, 2022] has emerged as a state-of-the-art approach within diffusion-based generative modeling for learning and sampling high-dimensional data, including images [Esser et al., 2024], and has been applied broadly across a range of other domains [Lipman et al., 2024b]. Flow matching is attractive in part because it cleanly separates three design choices: (i) the

This modularity has led to a growing ecosystem of variants, including conditional flow matching constructions that leverage conditional distributions and couplings to define tractable target velocities [Tong et al., 2024]. Empirically, these methods have been applied to high-dimensional generation tasks such as images, audio, and video [Lipman et al., 2023, 2024a, Guan et al., 2024, Jin et al., 2025], and have also been used for conditional generation and editing, where one integrates an ODE while enforcing constraints (e.g., classifier guidance, inverse problems, or conditioning variables) [Dhariwal and Nichol, 2021, Ho and Salimans, 2022, Song et al., 2022, Chung et al., 2023]. Beyond generation, learned continuous-time transports are increasingly used as primitives for data interpolation, domain translation, and simulation of complex dynamical systems [Liu et al., 2022, Tong et al., 2024, Chen et al., 2018]. Despite this empirical success, a theoretical understanding of flow matching remains incomplete. There are three intertwined sources of error that ultimately determine the quality of generated samples: (1) optimization error from training neural networks with nonconvex objectives using first-order methods, (2) statistical error from learning the velocity field from finitely many samples, and (3) sampling error from approximating the continuous-time dynamics with a neural network learned velocity field. Hence, the questions that we address in this work can be summarized as follows: 1. What is the convergence guarantee for the neural network training with gradient descent ? 

2. What is the generalization guarantee for the velocity field matching ? 

3. What is the distributional guarantee for samples generated by neural-network-based flow match-ing? 

Contributions. To address the three questions above, we summarize our main contributions as follows. 1. We establish convergence guarantees for approximating high-dimensional conditional velocity fields using gradient-descent-trained two-layer neural networks. Our analysis connects the training dynamics to the neural tangent kernel (NTK) regime [Du et al., 2018, Jacot et al., 2018] and addresses the challenges posed by high dimensionality, including regimes where the 2data dimension d is of the same order as the sample size n.2. We derive L2 statistical error bounds for the velocity field learning problem by introducing a general high-dimensional multi-task learning generalization bound for unbounded losses. This result complements existing representation learning theory [Maurer, 2016, Maurer et al., 2016] and may be of independent interest. 3. We provide sampling error guarantees for flow matching in Wasserstein distance. The proof combines a geometric lemma that upgrades L1 control to L‚ąě control on a compact high-dimensional subset with a refined analysis of the induced ODE dynamics. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first sampling error guarantee for gradient-descent-trained, neural-network-based flow matching algorithms. 4. Our theoretical results are complemented by numerical experiments on both the synthetic and real-world images that validate the convergence and sampling error bounds. These experiments provide insights into the practical implications of our theoretical findings and highlight potential avenues for future research. 

Organization. The remainder of this work is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related work and positions our contributions relative to existing literature. Section 3 introduces the main definitions and summarizes our results. Section 4 reports numerical experiments that complement the theoretical analysis. Finally, Section 5 discusses limitations and directions for future work. 

# 2 Related Works 

Our work is closely related to two branches of recent theoretical research on generative modeling and overparameterized neural networks. In the following, we first review recent theoretical developments for flow matching models, and then discuss the techniques most directly related to those used in this work. 

Theoretical works on generative modeling algorithms. Recent works [Fukumizu et al., 2024, Su et al., 2025a,b] study statistical convergence rates for variants of flow matching. In particular, Fukumizu et al. [2024] shows that flow matching achieves near-optimal rates in the p-Wasserstein distance, comparable to diffusion models. However, their estimator is not neural-network-based, whereas neural networks are the primary object of study in our work. Su et al. [2025a,b] investigate the learning error of variants of neural-network-based flow matching, but their analysis focuses on the 3theoretical limits of certain neural network classes and does not address achievability by a concrete optimization procedure. In contrast, the neural network solution we study is achievable via gradient descent with polynomial-time complexity. Han et al. [2024] analyzes optimization and generalization for score matching in diffusion models using shallow neural networks. Their work does not provide distributional guarantees for samples generated from the learned score. Moreover, their two-layer network treats only the first layer weights {wr}r‚ąą[n] as trainable parameters, whereas our analysis allows weights from both the first and second layers (a.k.a. {ar}r‚ąą[n] and {wr}r‚ąą[n]) to be trainable. This distinction substantially complicates the analysis and renders several arguments in Han et al. [2024] inapplicable in our setting. Overall, much of the existing theory for flow matching is largely algorithmically agnostic, leaving a gap between statistical guarantees and practical learning procedures with polynomial-time complexity. In comparison, our analysis provides performance guarantees for gradient descent and bridges this gap by establishing algorithmic achievability. 

Convergence and generalization guarantees for overparameterized neural networks. Asubstantial body of work studies training dynamics and generalization in the overparameterized regime. Part of our analysis is motivated by recent advances connecting trained overparameterized neural networks to the neural tangent kernel (NTK) [Jacot et al., 2018]. In particular, a series of works establish convergence guarantees for gradient descent when training neural networks in the NTK regime [Li and Liang, 2018, Du et al., 2019b, Allen-Zhu et al., 2019b, Du et al., 2019a, Chizat et al., 2019, Zou et al., 2019, Zou and Gu, 2019, Lee et al., 2019]. Closely related studies also develop NTK-based generalization bounds for overparameterized neural networks [Allen-Zhu et al., 2019a, Arora et al., 2019a,b, Cao and Gu, 2019, Ji and Telgarsky, 2020, Chen et al., 2021]. Another line of work leverages mean-field techniques to analyze the training of overparameterized shallow networks [Mei et al., 2018, Chizat and Bach, 2018, Mei et al., 2019, Wei et al., 2019], deep networks [Lu et al., 2020, Fang et al., 2021, Ding et al., 2021, 2022], and transformers [Gao et al., 2024]. While these studies offer valuable insights and theoretical tools for deep learning, they are largely developed for standard supervised learning settings. # 5 Discussion 

We discuss the limitations and a few open questions for future investigation. The first question we did not manage to answer is the sharpness of the bound given by the theorems in Section 3. To address this issue, one may need to construct a few example distributions that are hard to learn with 14 flow matching algorithms. Another direction is that we only consider a two-layer neural network, and it is worth investigating whether deeper neural networks might exhibit different convergence behavior and distribution-learning properties. Finally, we only consider the simple gradient descent algorithm. In the real world applications, researchers normally utilize stochastic gradient descent methods or the Adam optimizer for optimizing deep neural networks. Whether these methods can yield similar results, or even provide better convergence guarantees for the learning procedure, is also worth exploring.
