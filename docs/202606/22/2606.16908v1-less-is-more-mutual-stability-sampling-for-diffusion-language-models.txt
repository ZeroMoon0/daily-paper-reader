# LESS Is More: Mutual-Stability Sampling for Diffusion Language Models 

Amr Mohamed 1,2 *, Guokan Shang 1, Michalis Vazirgiannis 1,2

Abstract 

Diffusion large language models (dLLMs) offer a promising alternative to autoregressive decod-ing by iteratively refining masked sequences, enabling parallel token updates and bidirec-tional conditioning. Their practical efficiency, however, is limited by sampling procedures that execute a fixed number of reverse denoising steps selected before decoding, spending com-putation on already-stable positions and some-times committing unstable ones too early. We present LESS, a training-free, model-agnostic adaptive sampler that treats token commitment as an online stopping problem. LESS im-plements mutual-stability sampling through a joint stability rule that makes a masked po-sition eligible for unmasking only when its top-1 prediction has high confidence, its top-1 token persists across recent reverse steps, and its predictive distribution is stable under top-K inter-step Jensen–Shannon divergence. We evaluate LESS on Dream-7B, LLaDA-8B, and LLaDA-1.5-8B, covering full-sequence diffusion and semi-autoregressive blockwise sampling regimes, across seven benchmarks spanning general knowledge, math, and code. LESS improves average accuracy over strong training-free adaptive samplers while using 

72 .1% fewer reverse steps than fixed-budget decoding. Since each reverse step requires a Transformer forward pass, these step-count re-ductions translate into fewer forward evalua-tions, lower measured wall-clock latency, and lower estimated inference compute. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have advanced rapidly, yet text generation remains dominated by autoregressive (AR) decoding, limiting opportu-nities for global refinement (Brown et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025). Diffusion large language models (dLLMs) have emerged

as a compelling alternative: instead of generat-ing one token at a time, they iteratively denoise masked sequences, enabling parallel token updates, bidirectional conditioning over partially generated text, and natural infilling (Zou et al., 2023). This paradigm has matured quickly, with recent work training capable diffusion LMs from scratch (Nie et al., 2026; Ye et al., 2025b; Zhu et al., 2025), adapting AR checkpoints to diffusion objectives (Gong et al., 2025), and extending dLLMs to rea-soning, planning, and code-generation (Zhao et al., 2026; Ye et al., 2025a; Song et al., 2025; Labs et al., 2025). Together with scaling evidence (Nie et al., 2025), these advances position dLLMs as practical language modeling architectures. Despite this promise, decoding efficiency re-mains a central bottleneck for dLLMs. Generation proceeds through a reverse-diffusion chain with many refinement steps, and standard samplers re-quire the reverse-step budget and unmasking sched-ule to be chosen a priori , often conservatively to avoid quality loss across tasks (Nie et al., 2026; Ye et al., 2025b; Zhu et al., 2025). This fixed schedule ignores position-wise convergence: empirical stud-ies of adaptive dLLM decoding show that some token predictions stabilize well before the final reverse step (Li et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2026; Mo-hamed et al., 2025). As a result, some positions continue to be recomputed after their local predic-tive distributions have stabilized, while others may be unmasked before those distributions have set-tled. Since most dLLM samplers treat unmasked tokens as fixed, premature commitment is difficult to recover from: once an incorrect token is written, later reverse steps typically cannot revise it. A growing body of work addresses this bottle-neck from complementary directions. Training-time methods shorten the reverse process through distillation, curricula, or flow matching (Sahoo et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Monsefi et al., 2025), while systems methods reduce per-step latency 1

through caching or speculative execution (Ma et al., 2026; Jiang et al., 2025; Agrawal et al., 2025; Gao et al., 2025). Training-free samplers instead adapt when positions are unmasked, using confidence, margin, or inter-step divergence signals from the model’s predictive distributions (Li et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2026; Wu et al., 2025; Mohamed et al., 2025). These methods show that predictions often stabilize before the final diffusion step, but leave open a fundamental question: when is a masked position stable enough to stop refining and commit? 

We address this question by framing adap-tive unmasking as per-position online stopping. Prior training-free samplers provide partial crite-ria: confidence- and margin-based methods test whether the current prediction is locally decisive, while divergence-aware methods test whether con-secutive predictive distributions are close (Li et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Mohamed et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2026). LESS extends this line by treating stability as a joint property of the current prediction and its recent reverse-time trajectory. It implements mutual-stability sampling with a joint rule: a po-sition is eligible for unmasking only when its top-1 prediction is confident, its top-1 token persists across recent reverse steps, and its top-K inter-step Jensen–Shannon divergence is small. The top-K inter-step Jensen–Shannon diver-gence (JSD) is the key distributional signal in LESS. It provides a bounded, symmetric measure of how much a position’s predictive distribution changes between reverse steps. This matters as con-fidence alone cannot reveal ongoing distributional motion, and a fixed top-1 token can hide redistri-bution among plausible alternatives. Together, the three signals yield a training-free, model-agnostic joint stability rule computed from predictive distri-butions already produced during decoding. We evaluate LESS on three dLLMs across seven math, code, and general-knowledge benchmarks. LESS improves the accuracy–efficiency trade-off on average over strong training-free adaptive sam-plers, with the most pronounced gains on math and code. Ablations show that confidence is the primary acceptance signal, while persistence and inter-step JSD provide complementary safeguards. FLOPs profiling and wall-clock measurements con-firm that step reductions translate into fewer Trans-former forward evaluations and lower decoding latency. Our contributions are: • We formulate dLLM token commitment as a per-position online stopping problem induced by irreversible unmasking. • We introduce top-K inter-step Jensen– Shannon divergence as a bounded, symmetric stability signal for dLLM decoding. • We present LESS, a training-free sampler that combines confidence, top-1 token persistence, and inter-step JSD in a joint stability rule. • We validate LESS across three dLLM fami-lies and seven benchmarks, showing improved average accuracy–efficiency trade-offs with measured compute savings. LESS reduces reverse denoising steps through training-free, stability-gated unmasking. Our code is publicly available. 1

22 Related Work 

Masked diffusion language models. Masked diffusion language models generate text by itera-tively denoising partially masked sequences, expos-ing per-position predictive distributions at each re-verse step. Recent dLLMs such as LLaDA, Dream, and LLaDA-1.5 support instruction following, rea-soning, and code generation (Nie et al., 2026; Ye et al., 2025b; Zhu et al., 2025). Related works study adaptation from AR checkpoints, scaling behavior, long-context extension, and code-specialized vari-ants (Gong et al., 2025; Nie et al., 2025; He et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025). These efforts position dLLMs as a practical alternative to AR generation across diverse generation settings, but inference remains dominated by reverse denoising steps. 

Efficient dLLM inference. Prior acceleration methods reduce this cost in three complementary ways. Training-time methods shorten the reverse process through curricula, consistency-style dis-tillation, or flow matching (Sahoo et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Monsefi et al., 2025); systems methods reduce per-step cost through diffusion-aware caching, speculative decoding, or related reuse mechanisms (Ma et al., 2026; Jiang et al., 2025; Agrawal et al., 2025; Gao et al., 2025); and inference-time adaptive samplers change which po-sitions are unmasked and when . LESS belongs to the third class and is orthogonal to the first two: it reduces the number of reverse steps and can in prin-ciple be combined with faster per-step execution. 

Adaptive token commitment. Training-free adaptive samplers typically accelerate dLLM de-coding by committing tokens once a local reliabil-ity criterion is satisfied. Confidence-based rules un-mask positions whose top prediction or top-1/top-2 margin is large (Li et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Mohamed et al., 2025); these methods are effective when confidence is well aligned with convergence, but confidence alone does not explicitly track re-cent changes in the predicted token or continued motion of the predictive distribution. Divergence-aware methods address this limitation by adding inter-step stability checks, such as token-level KL between consecutive per-position predictive dis-tributions (Kim et al., 2026), although KL-based criteria are directional and unbounded. LESS ex-tends this line by treating adaptive unmasking as per-position online stopping: a position is accepted only when confidence, top-1 token persistence, and inter-step distributional stability agree. In particu-lar, LESS uses a bounded, symmetric top-K JSD criterion and adds an explicit persistence gate for the predicted token identity. To our knowledge, LESS is the first training-free dLLM sampler to use JSD as an inter-step stability signal for adaptive token commitment. 

3 Methods 

We first define the masked-diffusion decoding setup, then present LESS, a training-free adap-tive sampler that treats token-unmasking as a per-position online stopping problem. LESS imple-ments mutual-stability sampling through a joint stability rule over top-1 confidence, top-1 token persistence, and top-K inter-step JSD.

3.2 Adaptive unmasking as online stopping 

Because committed tokens are typically not revised, selecting Ut is an irreversible token-commitment decision. LESS therefore treats adaptive unmask-ing as a per-position online stopping problem. For each position i, the sampler observes a trajectory of predictive distributions over reverse steps and chooses a stopping time that is adapted to the in-formation available during decoding. Once i is unmasked, its selected token is carried forward by the reverse process and is not revised by later steps. The goal is to stop early enough to avoid recomput-ing positions whose local predictions have already stabilized, while avoiding premature commitments before the predictive trajectory has settled. LESS implements this stopping rule through three ob-servable stability signals: current top-1 confidence, persistence of the top-1 token over recent observa-tions, and inter-step distributional stability under top-K Jensen–Shannon divergence.

3.5 Unmasking policy 

The joint stability rule identifies eligible positions; the unmasking policy chooses among them. For full-sequence diffusion samplers, where positions may be selected across the full-generation span, LESS uses a frontier-first policy. Let 

ft := min Mt (21) be the leftmost still-masked position. If ft is el-igible, LESS unmasks it; otherwise, it scans a look-ahead window of size W and unmasks the first eligible position: 

j⋆ = min {j : ft < j ≤ ft + W, j ∈ M t,

accept t,j = 1 }. (22) If no eligible position is found, the sampler skips the step. After Π consecutive skipped steps, LESS performs a forced-unmasking step using the under-lying sampler’s default action. This fallback guar-antees termination but is not counted as accepted by the joint stability rule. For semi-autoregressive blockwise samplers, LESS applies the same eligi-bility rule inside the active block and preserves the model’s blockwise decoding interface.

5 Results 

Table 1 summarizes the main results across all three model families and seven benchmarks. LESS im-proves the aggregate accuracy–efficiency trade-off over strong training-free adaptive samplers. Across the three model families, LESS improves or matches the category-macro mean relative to Base while using substantially fewer denoising steps. On Dream-7B, LESS reaches 65 .18 mean accuracy ( ∆+1 .88 ) with 64 .3 steps. On LLaDA-68B, it reaches 57 .41 mean accuracy ( ∆+0 .83 ) with

steps. Averaged across the three model-family means, LESS uses 72 .1% fewer reverse steps than Base while yielding average accuracy gains of

These gains are achieved with a fixed global configuration, indicating that the stability signals used by LESS transfer consistently across tasks, models, and decoding regimes. 

6 Ablations and Analysis 

We analyze LESS along four axes: the contri-bution of each stability signal to robust unmask-ing, the extent to which decoding is governed by rule-accepted unmaskings rather than the forced-unmasking fallback, the role of the unmasking pol-icy across decoding regimes, and whether reduc-tions in reverse steps translate into compute savings. Full ablation results are provided in Appendix E. 

6.1 Complementarity of the stability signals 

We test whether the three signals in the joint sta-bility rule provide complementary safeguards by comparing full LESS with single-signal and drop-one variants on Dream-7B and LLaDA-8B. Under the fixed global-threshold setting, no individual sig-nal provides the same reliable operating point as the full rule. Confidence is the strongest standalone sig-nal on average, while JSD-only, persistence-only, and margin-only exhibit substantially weaker ag-gregate performance across model families and benchmarks (Appendix Table 4). This indicates that JSD and persistence are not intended to act as standalone unmasking criteria; rather, they serve as stabilizing gates when combined with confidence. Drop-one variants show the same pattern in re-verse. Removing confidence causes the largest degradation, confirming its role as the primary ac-ceptance gate. Removing inter-step JSD or per-sistence is less damaging, but the resulting two-signal variants are less stable across models and benchmarks than the full rule (Appendix Table 5). These results support the conjunctive design of LESS: confidence provides decisiveness, persis-tence guards against recent top-1 changes, and JSD guards against continuing distributional motion. 

6.2 Rule-driven versus forced unmasking 

The forced-unmasking fallback guarantees progress when no position passes the joint stability rule by forcing at least one required position to be unmasked after Π consecutive skipped steps. In the full-sequence Dream-7B setting, where the rule can directly govern unmasking decisions, the fallback is invoked on only 2–9% of reverse steps across audited benchmarks. Thus, most Dream-7B reverse steps are governed directly by the joint stability rule rather than by fallback progress. We also audit top-1 token changes over reverse time. As shown for Dream-7B in Figure 2, the top-1 flip rate is highest early and decays steadily, reaching at most 5% in the final quarter of the executed trajectories. This supports the temporal-stability assumption underlying LESS: per-position predictive distributions tend to stabilize over reverse time, but early commitments can occur before that stabilization is complete. Further details are provided in Appendix E.6. 

6.3 Robustness across decoding regimes 

To isolate the effect of the unmasking policy, we compare the frontier-first policy with a parallel-unmasking variant that commits all eligible posi-tions at each reverse step. On Dream-7B, paral-lel unmasking lowers accuracy on three of four math/code benchmarks, with an average change of 

−2.7 accuracy points and little aggregate change in step count. This is consistent with the inter-pretation that committing many eligible positions simultaneously can perturb subsequent denoising states before local context has fully settled. On LLaDA-8B, accuracy is unchanged because its re-leased semi-autoregressive blockwise sampler al-ready commits accepted positions in parallel within the active block. Additional budget and token-selection ablations show that T = 256 reaches the rule’s operating point for the evaluated settings: increasing to T = 512 produces no material aggre-gate improvement, reducing to T = 128 degrades harder settings, and nucleus sampling affects ac-curacy without changing the eligibility criterion at each reverse step. Further details are provided in Appendix E.

These results indicate that the efficiency gains come from step-count reduction rather than per-step acceleration. 

7 Discussion 

As diffusion LLMs become increasingly practi-cal alternatives to autoregressive generation, in-ference efficiency becomes central to their real-world applicability. LESS addresses this chal-lenge by committing tokens only when their pre-dictions are confident, temporally persistent, and distributionally stable. Our results show that this stability-gated commitment improves the aggregate accuracy–efficiency trade-off while reducing infer-ence compute, with the most pronounced gains on math and code generation tasks. 

Stability is more than confidence. The central premise of LESS is that irreversible token commit-ment should depend on stability at the time of com-mitment, not solely confidence. A high-confidence predictive distribution can still be unreliable if the top-1 token has recently changed or if probability mass is still moving among plausible alternatives. The ablations in Section 6 support this decompo-sition: confidence provides the main selectivity signal, while top-1 token persistence and inter-step JSD improve robustness by guarding against recent top-1 flips and continuing distributional motion be-fore local predictions have fully settled. 

Why inter-step JSD is useful. The top-K inter-step JSD provides a practical notion of distribu-tional stability between consecutive token-level predictive distributions. Unlike one-way KL crite-ria, it does not require choosing a reference step. Its bounded scale gives the JSD threshold a sta-ble numerical range across positions and timesteps, and its symmetry matches the sampler’s question: whether two consecutive beliefs are close enough to treat the current prediction as stable, even under changing partially decoded contexts. The top-K

projection focuses this comparison on tokens that can plausibly affect the unmasking decision while retaining remaining mass through a residual bin, without requiring full-vocabulary computations. 

Complementarity. LESS reduces the number of reverse denoising steps and is therefore com-plementary to methods that reduce per-step cost, such as diffusion-aware KV caching, speculative decoding, or systems-level reuse mechanisms. It is 8also complementary to training-time acceleration methods that shorten the reverse process through distillation or modified objectives. Because LESS uses only per-position predictive distributions al-ready emitted during decoding, it acts as a drop-in inference-time sampling policy rather than a re-placement for model-level or systems-level accel-eration, and can be combined with such methods without changing the underlying model. 

8 Conclusion 

We introduced LESS, a training-free adaptive sam-pler for diffusion language models that treats to-ken unmasking as per-position online stopping. LESS commits a masked position only when top-1 confidence, top-1 token persistence, and top-K

inter-step Jensen–Shannon divergence jointly in-dicate stability. Across Dream-7B, LLaDA-8B, and LLaDA-1.5-8B on seven benchmarks, LESS improves the accuracy–efficiency trade-off on aver-age over fixed-budget decoding and strong training-free adaptive samplers, with the largest gains on math and code. Averaged across the three model-family means, it uses 72 .1% fewer reverse steps than fixed-budget decoding while also yielding av-erage accuracy gains. Ablations confirm that the three signals provide complementary safeguards, and FLOPs profiling shows that step reductions translate into fewer Transformer forward passes, lower wall-clock time, and lower inference com-pute. These results support mutual stability as a practical inference-time criterion for robust early token commitment in efficient dLLM sampling. 

Limitations 

While LESS provides a training-free mechanism for accelerating diffusion language-model decod-ing, our study has several limitations. LESS exposes an explicit quality–efficiency trade-off through its stability thresholds, primarily the con-fidence threshold c and inter-step JSD threshold d.More conservative settings, with higher confidence requirements and lower JSD tolerance, reduce the risk of premature commitment but yield more mod-erate speedups. More permissive settings can accel-erate decoding further but may accept unstable posi-tions. In our experiments, this trade-off is resolved by selecting a fixed global configuration shared across the evaluated models and benchmarks. In practice, the optimal operating point may depend on the model, decoding horizon, task type, output length, and tolerance for quality degradation.

A Algorithm and Complexity 

Algorithm 1 gives the full LESS sampler. For each position that remains masked, the sampler maintains a short top-1-token history Hi and the previous top-K summary Qi of the predictive dis-tribution. At each reverse step, it forms the eligible set Et using the joint stability rule, selects an un-masking set Ut using the unmasking policy from Section 3.5, and applies a forced-unmasking fall-back after Π consecutive skipped steps. Given top-K summaries, the coarsened JSD computation is O(K) per masked position. The remaining operations consist of top-1-token-history checks, bounded-window scans, and constant-size state updates. These operations are negligible rela-tive to a Transformer forward pass; the measured speedups therefore come from executing fewer re-verse denoising steps rather than from reducing the cost of each step.

E.3 Unmasking Policy 

To isolate the effect of the unmasking policy, we compare the frontier-first policy used by full LESS against a parallel-unmasking variant that commits all eligible positions at each reverse step. The stabil-ity criteria are kept fixed at the per-(model, bench-mark) (c, d ) optimum. E.5 Token Selection 

The eligibility signals in LESS are computed from the model predictive distribution pt,i before token selection. We therefore vary the token-selection rule while keeping the eligibility rule fixed, com-paring argmax selection with two nucleus-sampling configurations for both model families. Table 8 shows that accuracy changes mainly reflect each model’s sensitivity to token selec-tion rather than changes to the eligibility criterion. Dream-7B is less sensitive to the tested nucleus-sampling variants than LLaDA-8B, which shows substantial degradation across all four benchmarks. The eligibility criterion itself is independent of the token-selection rule at each reverse step, since it operates on pt,i before any token is selected. 

E.6 Fallback Usage and Top-1 Token Stability 

We audit Dream-7B on 50 examples per benchmark at the per-setting tuned configuration. For each reverse step, we log whether the fallback is invoked, the number of positions unmasked, and the top-1 token at each position. The fallback is invoked only when no position is accepted by the joint stability rule at a given step; the forced-unmasking action then unmasks the frontier position. Across GSM8K, MATH, HumanEval, and MBPP, the fallback is invoked on 2.1% , 3.9% ,

8.9% , and 2.5% of reverse steps, respectively. Thus, most Dream-7B reverse steps are governed directly by the joint stability rule, and LESS acts primarily as a stability-gated stopping rule in the full-sequence setting. The per-step top-1 token flip rate on Dream-7B is highest early in decoding and falls to at most 5% in the final quarter of the trajectory across all audited benchmarks. This sup-ports the temporal-stability assumption underlying LESS: per-position predictive distributions tend to stabilize over reverse time, but early commitments can occur before that stabilization is complete.
